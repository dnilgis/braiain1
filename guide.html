How to Spot AI-Generated Images: 10 Detection Tips You Can Practice Today

AI-generated images are getting better every month. Here's how to train your eye to catch what the algorithms still get wrong — and a free daily game to practice.

In 2026, the best AI image generators produce photos that fool most people most of the time. A recent study found that even AI-savvy adults correctly identify AI-generated images only about half the time — essentially a coin flip. But detection is a skill, not a talent. Like a cashier who handles enough bills to feel a counterfeit the moment it touches their fingers, you can train your visual instincts. Here are 10 techniques that work, drawn from thousands of daily rounds played on BRAIAIN.

// ANATOMY & BIOLOGY
TIP 01
CHECK THE HANDS

Hands remain AI's most persistent failure point. While the days of six fingers are mostly over, look closer: fingers may be fused at the joints, bend at impossible angles, or have fingernails on both sides. The problem compounds when hands interact with objects — gripping a coffee mug, holding a phone, or clasping another hand. Count the fingers. Check the thumb placement. If someone is holding something, trace whether the grip makes physical sense.

AI has improved dramatically here since 2023, but complex hand poses — like interlaced fingers or a hand partially hidden behind an object — still trip up even the best models.

TIP 02
EXAMINE SKIN AND HAIR BOUNDARIES

Zoom into where hair meets background, or where skin meets clothing. In real photos, these transitions are complex — individual hairs catch light differently, collars cast tiny shadows, earlobes create specific silhouettes. AI tends to create a subtle smearing or haloing effect at these boundaries, blending the foreground into the background in a way that looks fine at thumbnail size but falls apart under scrutiny. Pay special attention to ears, hairlines, and the edges of glasses.

// TEXT & SYMBOLS
TIP 03
READ ANY TEXT IN THE IMAGE

This is the single most reliable tell. Any text visible in the image — on signs, book spines, clothing, screens, labels, or storefronts — deserves close reading. AI has gotten much better at rendering text, but it still produces subtle errors: letters that almost look right but aren't quite any real character, words that are close to English but are actually gibberish, or fonts that shift style mid-word. If the text is flawless, it doesn't prove the image is real — but if the text is garbled, it's almost certainly AI.

// PHYSICS & LOGIC
TIP 04
ANALYZE LIGHTING AND SHADOWS

Real photographs have a single set of physical light sources that affect every object consistently. AI doesn't understand light as physics — it understands it as a pattern. The result: shadows that point in different directions, reflections that don't match the scene, highlights on the wrong side of an object, or ambient light that's unnaturally even. Check whether shadows from different objects all point toward the same light source. Look at reflective surfaces — does the reflection match what should be there?

TIP 05
CHECK BACKGROUND LOGIC

AI fills backgrounds with things that look plausible but don't make sense. Buildings with staircases that lead nowhere. Shelves with objects that dissolve into blobs. A window view that's physically impossible given the room's architecture. Street scenes where the perspective shifts mid-image. The subject of an AI image is usually the most convincing part — the background is where the seams show. Don't just look at the subject. Study everything behind it.

// PATTERNS & TEXTURE
TIP 06
LOOK FOR REPETITIVE PATTERNS

Count similar objects: leaves on a tree, bricks in a wall, people in a crowd. AI loves to create subtle copy-paste patterns where similar elements are a little too similar — same angle, same lighting, same spacing. Nature doesn't repeat this precisely. Crowds are particularly telling: look for faces that are variations on the same face, or clothing that's suspiciously coordinated. Foliage is another weakness — AI renders it as a texture rather than individual plants, creating a uncanny uniformity.

TIP 07
ZOOM INTO TEXTURES

Real photographs maintain consistent detail at every zoom level — the weave of fabric, the grain of wood, the pores of skin all hold up under magnification. AI textures tend to dissolve: zoom in and you'll find areas of suspicious smoothness, repetitive micro-patterns, or detail that just... stops. The inconsistency is the tell. Part of the image might be hyper-detailed while an area nearby is inexplicably flat and blurry.

TIP 08
EXAMINE NOISE PATTERNS

Every real photograph has sensor noise — a subtle grain that's consistent across the entire image, created by the camera's sensor. AI-generated images either have no noise at all (unnaturally clean) or have noise that varies inconsistently across the image: clean in some areas, grainy in others, with no physical reason for the difference. This is easiest to spot in low-light scenes or solid-color areas like sky or walls.

// COMPOSITION & FEEL
TIP 09
CHECK SYMMETRY AND PROPORTION

AI tends to create compositions that are suspiciously balanced and centered. Real photographs have natural asymmetry — a subject slightly off-center, imperfect framing, one eye catching slightly more light than the other. When everything in an image looks mathematically perfect — perfectly centered subject, perfectly balanced colors, perfectly even spacing — that perfection itself is a red flag. Real life is messy. AI defaults to tidy.

TIP 10
TRUST YOUR GUT, THEN VERIFY

Sometimes an image just feels off — a vague unease you can't pin to any single flaw. This instinct is real and trainable. Your visual cortex processes far more information than your conscious mind can articulate, and with practice, it gets better at flagging AI-generated images before you can explain why. The key is practice: the more real and AI-generated images you study, the sharper this intuition becomes. Like the cashier who can feel a counterfeit bill, you develop a sense for authenticity that goes beyond any checklist.

// THE BIGGER PICTURE

No single technique catches every AI-generated image. The best models in 2026 can produce images that pass most of these checks individually. The power is in combining them: an image might have perfect hands and flawless text, but the lighting is physically impossible and the background dissolves under zoom. Detection is a skill built through repeated exposure, not a trick you learn once. That's why daily practice matters more than any single tip on this page.

As AI image generation continues to advance, the techniques that work today may not work tomorrow. But the fundamental skill — careful, skeptical visual analysis — will always matter. The question isn't whether AI will get better. It's whether we'll keep up.
